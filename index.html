Automation & Information Management

I use automation because I‚Äôm tired of watching skilled people waste time on problems that should have been solved years ago.

My goal is not to impress with complex scripts.

My goal is to quietly remove friction from everyday project work.

Workflow Automation
I automate everyday project workflows that quietly consume time and attention. From issue creation and status updates to approvals, data validation and notifications, I remove manual steps wherever possible using Power Automate and custom scripts. These automations are designed to blend into existing processes, not replace them with another tool teams have to learn.
Model Health & Performance
I build systems that continuously monitor model health instead of checking it only when something breaks. This includes tracking file size growth, broken links, incorrect worksets, coordination risks and performance bottlenecks. The aim is early warning, not post-mortem analysis - catching problems while they are still cheap to fix.
Standards as Code
I treat standards as executable rules, not PDF documents. Naming conventions, parameter requirements and modelling rules are translated into automated checks and validation logic. This removes ambiguity, reduces enforcement overhead and ensures consistency without constant manual policing.
Decision Support Automation
I focus on automation that supports decision-making, not just production. By connecting model data with dashboards and structured reporting, I help project leaders understand risk, progress and quality in near real-time. Automation becomes a strategic tool - not just an efficiency boost.

Information management is where most AEC projects still struggle.

We generate enormous amounts of data, yet decision-making remains slow, manual and risky.

I work in this space because I‚Äôm frustrated by how little of that data is actually used.

001 BIM Performance Dashboard
Surrey-Langley SkyTrain
2024
PowerBIPower AutomateDynamoACC

Challenge:

The project involved the delivery of 8 SkyTrain stations in Vancouver, with dozens of subcontractors across architecture, structures, civil, MEP, landscape, signalling, fire protection and more.
Although BIM standards were formally defined (BEP, MIDP, TIDP), they were applied inconsistently. Each organisation brought its own habits, naming conventions and modelling approaches. Information was fragmented, poorly standardised and often lost between tools and teams.
Technology existed, but processes were weak and people struggled to work in a truly digital way.

Idea:

Create a single, central source of truth that would continuously reflect the real BIM health of the project.
The solution had to work independently of human discipline, automate data flows as much as possible, and clearly compare expected versus actual information at object, model and project level.
Ideally, the tool would also act as a practical guide for less experienced engineers, showing not only what is wrong, but what the correct value should be.

Solution:

The BIM Performance Dashboard - an interactive Power BI report available to all project stakeholders.
Custom tools were developed to automatically extract and validate data from Revit and Civil 3D models on a weekly basis. Model content was checked against project requirements and compared directly with a master spreadsheet containing approved standards and values. Any deviation was immediately flagged and visualised.
In parallel, data from Autodesk Construction Cloud (used as the CDE) was automatically exported, including information containers, coordination issues, users and clashes. Using Power Automate, this data was pushed to SharePoint and connected live to the Power BI dashboard.

Why it mattered:

The dashboard transformed BIM from a static compliance exercise into a measurable, transparent performance system.
It significantly reduced coordination effort, exposed issues early, improved standard adoption across teams and created a shared understanding of what ‚Äúgood BIM‚Äù actually looks like on a complex, multi-disciplinary infrastructure project.
002 Revit Clash Guard
Surrey-Langley SkyTrain
2024
RevitC#

Challenge:

Despite active clash detection processes, very little action was taken on the results.
Interdisciplinary collaboration was weak and clashes were treated as a BIM problem rather than a design problem. Communication relied heavily on emails and manual coordination, leading to an accumulation of nearly 100,000 unresolved clashes that ultimately had to be reduced to zero.

Idea:

Clashes should be prevented at the moment they are created, not weeks later in coordination meetings.
If designers could immediately see that they were colliding with another discipline while modelling, many issues could be avoided altogether. The solution needed to work directly inside Revit and require minimal effort from the user.

Solution:

Clash Guard - a custom Revit add-in.
After defining basic parameters and selecting relevant linked models, the tool actively monitors modelling activity. When a clash occurs, the user is immediately notified via a pop-up.
The tool allows filtering by categories, worksets and disciplines, and provides access to a live list of detected clashes, enabling designers to resolve issues while they are still local and easy to fix.

Why it mattered:

Clash Guard shifted responsibility back to where it belongs - at the point of design.
It dramatically reduced the creation of new clashes, improved interdisciplinary awareness and helped change team behaviour from reactive coordination to proactive design quality.

Why it mattered:

The MIDP Engine turned information management into a scalable, auditable and user-friendly process. It reduced risk, improved data governance and allowed the project team to manage complexity with confidence at a critical pre-procurement stage.

005 Compass
Autodesk Consulting
2022
PythonGIS

Challenge:

There was no practical tool on the market capable of quickly generating and comparing road corridor variants using combined BIM and GIS constraints. Existing solutions were either too rigid, too slow or lacked meaningful scenario analysis capabilities.

Idea:

Develop a lightweight, software-agnostic tool based on GIS data that allows rapid corridor option generation and evaluation at early project stages.

Solution:

Compass - a Python-based application operating on an interactive world map. The tool consumes SHP files provided by clients or sourced from open data repositories, visualises constraints and allows users to define corridor parameters, start/end points and intermediate connections. Users can assign weights to constraints, choose optimisation goals (cost, speed, safety) and generate multiple route variants, visualised as previews or heatmaps, and exported back to SHP for further detailed design.

Why it mattered:

Compass enabled informed decision-making at concept stage, reducing time spent on unviable options and creating a clear digital bridge between GIS analysis and detailed BIM design.

007 Dynamo + Revit Bridge Generator
Lower Thames Crossing
2018
RevitDynamoC#

Challenge:

21 bridge models had to be produced in Revit within two weeks for public consultation visuals. Each structure was different, spatially complex and curved, with only three people available.

Idea:

Automate geometry creation using parametric inputs instead of manual modelling.

Solution:

A Dynamo-driven bridge generator using Excel as the input layer. Non-technical users populated basic parameters, while the script generated abutments, girders, decks, bearings, piers, barriers and railings, including automatic plans and sections. Modelling time was reduced from days to minutes. The tool was later rewritten in C# as an internal Revit add-in.

Why it mattered:

The generator proved how automation can fundamentally change delivery speed and consistency, even under extreme time pressure.
008 Revit Adaptive Family Automation
Dubai Metro Route 2020
2017
RevitExcelDynamo

Challenge:

A 15 km elevated metro line with over 450 piers and frequent alignment changes made manual re-modelling unsustainable.

Idea:

Separate geometry logic from location and dimensional data.

Solution:

A master spreadsheet storing coordinates, elevations and dimensions, combined with standard and adaptive Revit families. A Dynamo script regenerated elements automatically whenever inputs changed, eliminating repetitive manual updates.

Why it mattered:

The approach turned constant change from a major risk into a manageable, repeatable process and marked the foundation of a long-term focus on BIM automation.
